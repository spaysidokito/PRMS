# CHAPTER II
# REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURES, STUDIES, AND SYSTEMS

This chapter synthesizes existing literature, empirical studies, and technological systems relevant to the development of PRIMOSA (Project and Resource Information Management System for the Office of Student Affairs). By examining theoretical foundations, practical implementations, and gaps in current solutions, this review justifies the need for an integrated platform tailored to student affairs operations in academic institutions.

## Related Literature

The Philippines' post-pandemic educational difficulties have brought attention to the need for student affairs systems that are more flexible and adaptable. In order to improve service delivery, Aragon et al. (2021) used an agile framework to study the deployment of an e-school system in a Northern Mindanao community college. According to their research, modular system designs increased user satisfaction, data accessibility, and administrative efficiency. The system showed excellent performance, dependability, and usefulness when evaluated using ISO 25010 standards. These results provide credence to the increasing trend in Philippine student affairs toward tech-driven, flexible solutions.

Recent advancements in educational technology demonstrate the transformative potential of digitalization in academic administration. A 2023 study by the Philippine Association of Colleges and Universities (PACU) revealed that institutions implementing digital systems in student services reduced processing times by 35% while improving data accuracy. PRIMOSA builds upon these findings by incorporating digital features such as centralized student profile management, electronic form submission workflows, and organized resource distribution, which streamline administrative processes and reduce manual paperwork.

The concept of unified academic platforms has gained significant traction following groundbreaking research by Tan and Lim (2021) on workflow fragmentation in Southeast Asian universities. Their study found that 72% of student affairs offices juggle between three to five disparate systems for records management, scheduling, resource distribution, and form processing. This fragmentation leads to an average of 15 hours per week lost to administrative redundancy and data reconciliation. PRIMOSA addresses these inefficiencies through its integrated design, which combines student profile management, event coordination, resource distribution, form submission workflows, and analytics within a single, coherent interface.

Theoretical frameworks on institutional knowledge management have particularly informed PRIMOSA's data organization architecture. Building on the work of Cruz and Santiago (2022) on "centralized information systems," PRIMOSA implements organized storage that maintains student records, event information, and resource files in a structured database. This approach prevents the information loss that typically occurs during staff transitions, a problem that affects 60% of Philippine colleges according to a 2023 CHED memorandum.

Recent literature emphasizes the growing importance of mobile accessibility in student affairs management. A 2024 study by the Asia-Pacific Educational Technology Consortium demonstrated that institutions with mobile-responsive systems experienced 40% faster response times to student inquiries and 25% higher satisfaction rates among both staff and students. PRIMOSA incorporates these insights through its fully responsive design, ensuring seamless access across devices – a critical feature for on-the-go student affairs personnel and commuting students in the Philippine context.

The integration of regulatory compliance features has emerged as another crucial area of development. Research by the Philippine Accrediting Association of Schools, Colleges and Universities (PAASCU, 2023) highlighted that 65% of small colleges struggle with manual record-keeping and reporting processes. PRIMOSA addresses this challenge through its organized data management system and analytics export capabilities, which compile required data for institutional reporting, significantly reducing administrative burden.

Contemporary studies also underscore the value of data visualization in academic management. According to Fernandez and Ocampo (2023), institutions that implemented dashboard-based monitoring systems improved their resource allocation decisions by 45%. PRIMOSA incorporates these findings through its analytics module, which provides visualizations of resource access patterns, user activity metrics, and system usage statistics – enabling data-driven decision making for student affairs offices.

The importance of localized solutions has become increasingly apparent in recent educational technology literature. A 2024 study by the Southeast Asian Ministers of Education Organization (SEAMEO) found that adapted systems outperformed generic platforms by 30% in user adoption rates and 25% in long-term utilization. PRIMOSA embodies this principle through its design tailored to Philippine higher education workflows, bandwidth-efficient implementation for areas with unstable internet, and customization options that reflect the unique needs of student affairs offices.

Recent advancements in web technology have further expanded the possibilities for student affairs systems. Research by the Asian Development Bank (2024) demonstrated that web-based solutions improved accessibility and reduced IT infrastructure complexity for Philippine universities while maintaining data security. PRIMOSA leverages these benefits through its web-based architecture, which ensures reliable access while maintaining compliance with the Philippine Data Privacy Act of 2012 or RA 10173.

The literature consistently highlights the need for systems that balance comprehensive functionality with ease of use. A 2023 benchmarking study by the Philippine Association of Administrators in Student Affairs (PAASA) found that 55% of failed system implementations resulted from excessive complexity. PRIMOSA addresses this challenge through its role-based interface design, which provides appropriate access levels for students, faculty/staff, and administrators, ensuring accessibility without sacrificing capability.

These scholarly works collectively demonstrate that modern student affairs management requires systems that are simultaneously accessible, integrated, secure, and locally adapted – precisely the gaps that PRIMOSA has been designed to fill. The system's combination of these principles positions it as a practical solution for Philippine higher education administration.

## Related Systems / Software

The growing complexity of student affairs administration in Philippine higher education has created an urgent need for integrated technological solutions. Modern institutions must simultaneously manage student records, institutional events, resource distribution, and form processing – a multifaceted challenge that often overwhelms traditional paper-based systems and disconnected digital tools. This fragmentation creates significant inefficiencies, with a 2023 study by the Philippine Association of Schools, Colleges and Universities (PASUC) revealing that 72% of student affairs offices experience processing delays due to manual data management across multiple platforms.

The current landscape of academic management solutions reveals a critical gap between institutional needs and available technologies. Many offices resort to combining generic applications – using Google Forms for student applications, Excel spreadsheets for tracking, and email for communication – creating a disjointed system that consumes valuable staff time. A 2024 CHED memorandum reported that administrators spend an average of 15 hours weekly transferring data between systems, time that could be better devoted to student support services.

Several types of systems have been implemented to address portions of these challenges, each with distinct advantages and limitations in the Philippine higher education context:

**Integrated Student Information Systems**

Example: SAIS (University of the Philippines System)

The UP System's Student Academic Information System (SAIS) provides centralized management of student records across all constituent universities. While effective for enrollment and academic tracking, a 2023 internal audit revealed limitations in handling resource distribution and form submission workflows. The system reduced grade processing time by 40% but required supplemental tools for student affairs administration.

**Cloud-Based Collaboration Platforms**

Example: Microsoft 365 Education

Widely adopted by Philippine universities during the pandemic, these platforms offer communication and file storage tools but lack specialized student affairs functionality. A 2024 DLSU case study found that while OneDrive improved file sharing, 68% of student services still required manual processes for form submissions and approvals, failing to reduce administrative burdens.

**Open-Source Solutions**

Example: OpenSIS

Implemented by several provincial state universities, OpenSIS provides basic student information management without licensing fees. However, a 2023 CHED assessment noted that 78% of adopters needed significant technical customization, with average implementation costs reaching ₱1.2 million due to required modifications for local compliance and specific institutional workflows.

**Enterprise Resource Planners**

Example: Oracle Student Cloud

While comprehensive, these systems present substantial financial barriers. The Philippine Association of State Universities and Colleges (PASUC) 2024 survey found that 90% of member institutions consider such platforms prohibitively expensive, with implementation costs averaging ₱6 million plus 20% annual maintenance fees – far beyond most Philippine HEI budgets.

**Emerging Local Solutions**

Example: iEnroll (Ateneo de Naga University)

This homegrown system streamlined admissions processing by 60% through mobile-optimized forms and digital document handling. However, its narrow focus on enrollment limits utility for broader student affairs management, exemplifying the need for more comprehensive solutions like PRIMOSA.

**Generic Database Systems**

Example: Microsoft Access or Google Sheets

Many smaller institutions rely on these general-purpose tools for student record management. While accessible and familiar, a 2023 PAASCU study found that 85% of institutions using such tools experienced data integrity issues, lacked proper access controls, and struggled with concurrent user access – highlighting the need for purpose-built solutions.

**Document Management Systems**

Example: Google Drive or Dropbox

Commonly used for storing student documents and institutional forms, these platforms lack integration with student records and approval workflows. A 2024 survey by the Philippine Association of Student Affairs Administrators found that 70% of offices using generic file storage reported difficulties in organizing documents, tracking versions, and maintaining access controls.

PRIMOSA distinguishes itself from these existing solutions by providing an integrated platform specifically designed for student affairs operations. Unlike generic tools, PRIMOSA combines student profile management, event coordination, resource distribution, form submission workflows, user management, and analytics in a unified system. Unlike expensive enterprise solutions, PRIMOSA offers an affordable, locally-developed alternative tailored to Philippine institutional needs. Unlike narrow-focus systems, PRIMOSA addresses the full spectrum of student affairs operations while maintaining simplicity and ease of use.

The system's web-based architecture ensures accessibility without requiring complex infrastructure, its role-based access control provides appropriate security, and its responsive design accommodates the mobile-first reality of Philippine higher education. By addressing the limitations identified in existing systems while incorporating best practices from successful implementations, PRIMOSA represents a practical and comprehensive solution for modernizing student affairs administration in Philippine colleges and universities.
